What changed on Liquidity

The vitals band is the live page, untouched. The page already derives net reserves and the swap stock — some of the most careful arithmetic on the site — and then draws them as one line among three and says nothing. The redesign reads that arithmetic out loud, adds the brief rows the page never had, and converts the evidence layer to the contract shipped on Overview and Deposits. Every figure below is live (W/E 3 July 2026).

The page’s best number was buried in a legend

Gross reserves are $159.7bn; net is $52.2bn; net excluding swaps is $34.5bn — 22% of the headline. Decomposed, the gross number is three different people’s money: $107.5bn is the banks’ own FX held as required reserves, $17.7bn is borrowed through CBRT swaps, and $34.5bn is the central bank’s own. That is now the lede, a flag with its rule, and the section that opens the evidence layer. Was: three lines on a chart called “gross, net, net excl. swaps”.

A stacked area would have lied here

The obvious way to draw “whose money is it” is a stack that sums to gross. It is wrong: the CBRT’s own net FX is negative in 42 of the last 150 weeks — as low as −$68.6bn in March 2024 — and a stack cannot draw a negative band without misstating the total. The chart is therefore three lines with the two gaps shaded, and a zero line the purple line spent a year underneath. The mark had to change because the data said so, which is the whole argument for building mockups on real series.

Two series that were never compared

The page plots residents’ FX savings and the CBRT’s reserves in different sections. Put on one axis: households hold $138.3bn in FX cash and gold — 3.1× the central bank’s net reserves on the same date, and the gold alone ($82.9bn) is 1.9× it. Residents’ FC is monthly and the reserves are weekly, so every ratio here is struck on the last date both publish — comparing a fresh weekly print with a month-old household number would flatter the central bank by half a turn.

Movers, Flags, the two systems, Ahead

Liquidity went from the vitals straight to “In depth” — on a page fed by a weekly bulletin and a weekly balance sheet, nothing said what moved this week. Seven rules now run (reserve quality, swap dependence, the TL deficit, the private LDR, the LCR/NSFR floors, dollarization); four fire, three print as clear. “The two systems” makes the public/private split — the page’s whole analytical frame — a table instead of a thing you infer from four charts.

The sections stop restating their axes

“TL Funding”, “FC & Dollarization”, “CBRT Liquidity & Reserves” become findings: the private banks lend out nearly every lira they take in (97% vs 81%); in FC the roles reverse (state 82% vs private 73%); the system has been short of lira for two years. Each chart carries a computed foot line, so a screenshot still argues. Was: five boxed Sections, titles that named the y-axis.

Kept

Every series: TL and FC loan-to-deposit by ownership, TL deposit growth (52w and 13w, sector and by ownership), dollarization by ownership, net CBRT funding, gross/net/ex-swap reserves, residents’ FC savings, LCR/NSFR/leverage, and the REER backdrop. The range selector stays. No new colour, no new component beyond what Overview and Deposits already added.

One thing I did not do

The vitals band still shows NSFR (123%, quarterly, sleepy) rather than net reserves. Reserves are a central-bank vital, not a bank one, and the band is the sector’s own vitals — so the buffer leads the brief instead, where it can carry its arithmetic. Worth arguing about.
